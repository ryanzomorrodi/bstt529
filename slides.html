Predicting Depression Among Veterans Using Health Care Data

Ryan Zomorrodi

April 7, 2025

Background

  • Depression is a major mental health challenge among U.S. veterans.
  • In 2008, Veterans Affairs (U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs 2016) estimated that among veterans visiting primary care clinics:
    • 1 in 3 veterans have some symptoms of depression
    • 1 in 5 have serious symptoms
    • 1 in 8 to 10 have major depression

Potential Causes of Depression Among Veterans

  • Exposure to violence, death, and trauma
  • Physical injuries (Veterans Affairs 2015)
  • Transition challenges (Bond et al. 2022)
  • Substance abuse (National Institute on Drug Abuse 2019)

Depression Prediction with Healthcare Data

  • Given the high prevalence of depression among veterans, it is import that healthcare providers pay special attention to their mental health.
  • A specialized prediction model for veterans may allow healthcare practitioners to identify veteran-specific characteristics that are associated with higher risk of depressive.

Data Background

  • 2005 to 2018 Centers for Disease Control and Prevention National health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES).
  • NHANES is a national representative survey conducted by the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS), which includes:
    • Health exams
    • Laboratory tests
    • Dietary interviews

Data Background (cont.)

  • The total dataset was filtered to include only veterans.
  • There are 3,929 samples and 490 predictors.
  • Special attention will be payed toward the memory issues and trouble sleeping predictors.

Bivariate Analysis

  • Depression is highest amongst young and middle-aged adults.
  • Depression is highest amongst people of color.

Bivariate Analysis

  • Depression is about 40% lower among married veterans.
  • Depression is much higher among those without a high school diploma.

Bivariate Analysis

  • Obesity is most prevalent amongst middle-aged individuals.

Bivariate Analysis

  • Younger veterans tend to have more education.

Methods - Logistic Regression

  • Imputation was performed with bagging
  • Upsampling was conducted with Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE)
  • Feature Selection was performed by utilizing Bayesian optimization to tune
    • Elastic Net
    • Random Forest
    • Light GBM
  • Voting method was used to only include model predictors within the top 50 most important in every model.
  • Backwards selection regression model was fit on the remaining 30 features.

Methods - Machine Learning

  • Feature Engineering
    • Combined work_type and out_of_work into one variable
    • Replaced all cancer variables with cancer_count
    • Combined arthritis_type and arthritis into one variable
  • Filtered features with near zero variation (categorical variables with the minority class with < 10 observations)
  • Split data into 75% training and 25% validation
  • Training data was up-sampled using SMOTE
  • Bagging imputation and normalization were performed as preprocessing to Elastic Net
  • Model selection was performed over 5 cross validated folds by utilizing Bayesian optimization to tune
    • Elastic Net
    • Random Forest
    • Light GBM

Limitations

  • Our sample is largely made up of older adults. Prediction performance may suffer for younger Iraq or Afgan War veterans.
  • Individuals with low healthcare access may be diagnosed at lower rates and thus, our sample may have an inherit bias present within it.
